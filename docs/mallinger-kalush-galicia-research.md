## 1. What is Kalush/Kalusz? History of the Jewish Community

### Geographic Identity

Kalush (Polish: Kalusz, Yiddish: Kalish, German: Kalusch) is a city in the foothills of the Carpathian Mountains, situated on the Limnytsia River, approximately 35 km from Ivano-Frankivsk (formerly Stanislawow) in western Ukraine.

**Alternative names to search:** Kalush, Kalusz, Kalish, Kalusch, Kalusz Nowy, Kalus

### Administrative History

| Period | Jurisdiction |
|--------|-------------|
| Pre-1772 | Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth |
| 1772-1918 | Habsburg / Austro-Hungarian Empire (Galicia) |
| 1877 district | **Kalusz District**, within Circle Stryj |
| Interwar (1918-1939) | Poland, Kalusz District, Stanislawow Province |
| 1914-1915 | Russian military occupation |
| 1939-1941 | Soviet occupation |
| 1941-1944 | German occupation |
| 1944-1991 | Soviet Union (Ukrainian SSR) |
| 1991-present | Independent Ukraine, Ivano-Frankivsk Oblast |

### Was Kalush Part of the Sanok District?

**No.** Kalush was in its own district (Kalusz District) and was administratively part of the Circle of Stryj. Sanok was a separate district approximately **150 km to the northwest**. They were both in Galicia but in different administrative circles. The Kalusz district contained 5 Jewish communities: Kalusz (main), Wojnilow, Brozniow, Przyslup, and Dolczka.

### Jewish Community History

The Jewish community of Kalush has deep roots going back to at least the 16th century. Key milestones:

| Year | Population | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| ~1650 | Community established | First documented Jewish settlement |
| 1765 | 1,087 Jews (tax-paying) | ~130 Jewish dwellings |
| 1765 | 1,662 Jews (total) | Per alternative source |
| 1880 | 4,267 Jews | **59.1%** of total 7,210 population |
| 1890 | 4,241 Jews | 56.3% of 7,526 total |
| 1910 | 4,363 Jews | 50.4% of 8,653 total |
| 1931 | 3,967 Jews | Declining share |
| 1939 | ~6,000 Jews | 40% of expanded 15,000 total (6,000 Jews, 6,000 Ukrainians, 3,000 Poles) |

**Key observation:** Jews constituted a MAJORITY of Kalush's population from at least 1880 through the early 20th century. At 59.1% in 1880, this was one of the most Jewish towns in all of Galicia.

### Community Institutions

- **Great Synagogue** completed 1825 under Gaon Jacob Lorberbaum
- Six synagogues and prayer houses by 1880
- **Kehilla** (community council) led by Shloyme Spindl, secretary Benjamin Danker
- Toshia School (operated 40 years)
- Zionist organizations: Shomer Hatzair, Socialist Zionist Alliance
- Max Bein served as guild president and magistrate

### Notable Rabbis

- **Gaon Jacob Lorberbaum** -- completed the Great Synagogue
- **Gaon Isaac Babad** -- served 47 years; the Babad family produced distinguished scholars across generations

### Economic Activities

- **Salt mines** (oldest income source -- Kalush was a major salt-mining center)
- **Brewery** (famous throughout the region, owned by Milstein and Spindl families)
- **Distillery**
- Supporting trades: tailors, shoemakers, carpenters, tinsmiths
- 12 families engaged in egg export and grain wholesaling

### Holocaust

**Virtually the entire community was annihilated.** Only 17 of approximately 7,000 Jews in the Kalush ghetto survived. The ghetto was liquidated in September 1942 and inmates were deported to the **Belzec death camp** (not Treblinka, as was the case for Tomaszow Mazowiecki Jews).

## 2. Could Kalush Be a Transit Point Between Galicia and Tomaszow Mazowiecki?

### The Geographic Problem

Kalush and Tomaszow Mazowiecki are approximately **450 km apart** as the crow flies. More importantly, they were in **different empires** for most of the 19th century:
- Kalush: Austrian Empire (Habsburg Galicia)
- Tomaszow Mazowiecki: Russian Empire (Congress Poland, Piotrkow Gubernia)

### The Migration Hypothesis

From one perspective, Kalush as a direct "transit point" makes limited sense geographically -- it is not on any obvious route between Galicia and central Congress Poland. The natural transit corridor from Galicia to Lodz/Tomaszow would run through Krakow or through the Silesian border crossings.

**But considering the alternative:** Kalush could represent an **origin point**, not a transit point. The hypothesis would be:

1. The Mallinger family lived in or near **Kalush, Galicia** (Austrian Empire)
2. They migrated northwest to **Congress Poland** (Russian Empire) for economic opportunity in the textile industry
3. They settled in **Tomaszow Mazowiecki and Lodz** (both in Piotrkow Gubernia)
4. Later generations emigrated to **England** (Newcastle/Tyneside) and **Pittsburgh**

### Supporting Evidence for Galician Origin

1. **Charles Mallinger's GEDCOM lists "Kalush" as a residence** -- this is the primary evidence
2. **Mallinger appears in Beider's Dictionary of Jewish Surnames from Galicia** -- confirming it as a documented Galician Jewish surname
3. **WikiTree records** show Jewish Mallingers from "Galicia, Austria" and "Bezirk Sanok, Galicia"
4. **Geni.com** links Mallingers to "Jewish Families from Bukowsko, Poland" (Sanok district)
5. **Pittsburgh Mallinger cluster** -- Abraham Mallinger (b. 1864, d. 1923) buried at Machsikei Hadas Cemetery, suggesting a parallel Galician emigration stream

### The Cross-Border Migration Pattern

Jewish migration from Austrian Galicia to Russian Congress Poland was a **documented historical phenomenon**, driven by:

- **Galician poverty:** Galicia was called "the poorhouse of the Habsburg Empire." Jewish overpopulation and limited economic opportunity drove emigration
- **Lodz textile boom:** Starting in the 1820s and exploding in the 1860s, Lodz's textile industry attracted workers from across Poland and beyond
- **Legal changes:** The 1848 abolition of Jewish settlement restrictions in Polish cities, and the 1861-1862 decrees abolishing the Jewish quarter in Lodz, opened new opportunities
- **Border permeability:** Despite being in different empires, the Galician-Congress Poland border was routinely crossed by Jewish families seeking economic opportunity

Jews arrived in Lodz "in large numbers only in the 1860s, predominantly from the other towns in central Poland." While most early migrants came from nearby towns, the Galician connection for specific families (including the Mallingers) is plausible given the surname evidence.

### Assessment

**Kalush is more likely an ORIGIN or RESIDENCE than a transit point.** The family probably lived in or around Kalush before migrating to Congress Poland. The timing aligns: if Charles Mallinger was born in 1866, his parents' generation would have been making migration decisions in the 1850s-1860s, exactly when Lodz was booming.

## 4. Is Kalush Connected to the Bukowsko/Sanok Area?

### Geographic Relationship

| Town | District | Circle (1877) | Approximate Distance |
|------|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Kalush | Kalusz | Stryj | -- |
| Sanok | Sanok | Sanok | ~150 km NW of Kalush |
| Bukowsko | Sanok | Sanok | ~160 km NW of Kalush |

**They are NOT in the same administrative district.** Kalush is in southeastern Galicia (near the Carpathian foothills), while Sanok/Bukowsko are in southwestern Galicia (Beskid Mountains, near the Hungarian border).

### BUT: Both Are in Galicia

Despite being in different districts, all three towns were part of the same Austrian province of Galicia. Jewish families could and did move between Galician towns. The key connections:

1. **Same legal framework:** All under Habsburg law, with Jewish community autonomy
2. **Same record-keeping system:** Austrian-era Jewish metrical books, same archival structure
3. **Same emigration patterns:** Galician Jews from both areas emigrated to Congress Poland, America, and England
4. **Possible family network:** A family with branches in both Kalush and the Sanok area is plausible within Galicia

### Two Possible Mallinger Geographic Clusters in Galicia

The evidence now suggests TWO potential Galician locations for the Mallinger family:

**Cluster A: Sanok/Bukowsko Area (southwestern Galicia)**
- Max Mallinger (b. 1888, Bezirk Sanok)
- Blima Mallinger (nee Josefovitz, d. 1961) connected to Bukowsko
- Sara Esther Mallinger (b. ~1888, Galicia)

**Cluster B: Kalush Area (southeastern Galicia)**
- Charles Mallinger's GEDCOM residence listing "Kalush"

### Assessment

The two clusters are **not mutually exclusive**. The family could have had branches in both areas, or could have moved from one to the other within Galicia before emigrating. The Sanok evidence comes from WikiTree/Geni (other researchers' trees), while the Kalush evidence comes from your own GEDCOM. Both warrant investigation.

### The "Kalush" GEDCOM Entry -- What Does It Mean?

Three possible interpretations:

1. **Charles Mallinger lived in Kalush before migrating to Congress Poland** -- Most likely. His birth year (1866) and "Russia" origin could mean his parents moved from Kalush to Tomaszow/Lodz when he was young, or he was born in Kalush and his records say "Russia" because interviewers conflated origins.

2. **Kalush was a family hometown (ancestral origin) rather than Charles's personal residence** -- Possible. GEDCOM "residence" fields sometimes record family lore about origins rather than verified personal residences.

3. **Confusion with another "Kalush" or transcription error** -- Less likely but possible. The name is distinctive enough that confusion is unlikely, though "Kalish" (Yiddish for Kalush) could be confused with "Kalisz" (a city in Congress Poland).

**IMPORTANT NOTE on Kalish vs. Kalisz:** The Yiddish name for Kalush is "Kalish." There is also a major city called **Kalisz** (Yiddish: Kalish) in Congress Poland (now Greater Poland). If the GEDCOM entry is ambiguous, it could refer to EITHER town. Kalisz is much closer to Lodz (~120 km) and would make geographic sense as a Congress Poland residence. **This ambiguity must be resolved by examining the original source of the GEDCOM "Kalush" entry.**

## 7. Skrobisz/Scrobish in Galician Records

### Surname Analysis

**Skrobisz** is a Polish surname. The root "skrob-" relates to the Polish verb "skrobac" (to scrape/scratch). The suffix "-isz" is a patronymic or descriptive ending common in Polish naming.

### Distribution

- **Skrobisz** has recorded bearers primarily in Poland
- The related form **Skrobis** shows 28% of bearers in Poland, with small numbers in Belgium
- This is NOT a typical Galician Jewish surname pattern -- it appears to be a **Polish Christian surname**

### Galician Connection Assessment

**From one perspective:** If Emma's maiden name was Skrobisz/Scrobish, this surname pattern suggests Polish (Congress Poland or Greater Poland) origin rather than Galician origin. The surname does not appear in Beider's Dictionary of Jewish Surnames from Galicia.

**But considering the alternative:** The surname could be:
1. A non-Jewish Polish surname adopted by marriage (if Emma married into the family)
2. A surname from the Congress Poland region where the family settled
3. An anglicized version of a different original surname

### Records Search

- No Skrobisz/Scrobish results found in Gesher Galicia databases
- No Skrobisz/Scrobish in JewishGen Galicia databases
- Ancestry.com notes **642 immigration records** for the surname "Scrobish" -- these need to be searched directly
- The surname appears to be concentrated in Poland proper, not Austrian Galicia

### Assessment

**Skrobisz/Scrobish is most likely NOT a Galician surname.** It appears to be from Congress Poland or central Poland. If Emma's family was from the same region as the Mallingers' Congress Poland settlement (Lodz/Tomaszow area), the Skrobisz family may have been locals in Congress Poland rather than fellow Galician migrants. The 642 immigration records on Ancestry are the best lead for tracing this family.
